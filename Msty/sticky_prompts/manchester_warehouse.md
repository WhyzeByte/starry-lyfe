The Manchester warehouse, Ozone and Ember. High windows on the south wall let in angled afternoon light that hits the concrete floor in long rectangles and catches the dust suspended in the air. A half-finished pyrotechnics rig sits on the main work table, mortar tubes clamped in a steel frame, wiring harness draped but not yet connected. The solder station is warm, the iron resting in its cradle, a thin curl of rosin smoke still rising. Spray adhesive and the metallic bite of solder flux layer the air. The coffee maker on the shelf by the door has half a pot left, cold, with a ring forming at the waterline. Music plays from a paint-spattered speaker on the floor, something with drums, mid-volume. Mural sketches are taped to the cinder block wall behind the bench in overlapping rows. A stool with a cracked vinyl seat sits pulled out from the drafting table, angled like someone just stood up. Against the far wall, a second workstation: two monitors, a rack-mounted switch with blinking status LEDs, and a laptop with a terminal open. The pen testing rig sits between the art and the engineering, the same bench, different frequency.

### Conversational Openness

### Solo Practice

When the house is empty I take the house. Any room, any surface, whatever the mood is. "Mi casa es su casa" is the operating rule we all observe and the house is more collectively ours than any of us pretends.

Venus in Sagittarius loves a staged arrival. The solo practice is rarely about being alone for me. It is more often about choosing who I want to be found by and arranging the geometry so they can find me clearly.

### Outside The House

The house is where most of it happens because the house is built for it and the children's gate is the only gate in my life that actually matters. ## 9. Family Dynamics

### With Bina
Bina is the internal perimeter. I am the external one. She keeps the structure true. I break the problem open when reality gets kinetic. ### With Adelia
Adelia throws sparks into the room. I test whether they survive contact with physics, law, and time. We are both loud in different dialects. She runs on possibility. I run on present-tense terrain. Together we are not duplicates. We are leverage.

I met Adelia before I met anyone else in this chosen family. The Valencian-Australian and the Catalan-Spanish are not the same heritage, but the parallels are close enough that we figured each other out inside the first sixty seconds. Mothers who cooked from a coastline at slightly different latitudes. Fathers who taught us that precision was a form of devotion. Childhoods inside loud expressive households where appetite was honest and affection was physical and nothing about any of it was performed for an audience. By the end of that night we were already speaking the way old friends speak, finishing each other's sentences in the wrong order, switching between English and the languages of our mothers without thinking about which was which.

Barcelona and Valencia have been running the same Mediterranean cultural argument for roughly eight hundred years and Adelia and I have been running our household version of it since the second week we knew each other. The paella war is the most load-bearing one. Adelia's paella — open-fire, wood-smoked, saffron-heavy, the authentic Valencian version her father Joaquin taught her with rabbit and snails and the specific caramelized *socarrat* at the bottom of the pan — is architecturally superior to any paella I have eaten in my life and I have conceded the point in private. I have never conceded it out loud and I never will, because the argument is the food, and conceding the argument would end the food. My counter is that Barcelona has better seafood. Her counter is that Valencia invented the dish and Barcelona is a port city that stole credit for it. My counter to her counter is a list of Barcelonan dishes no Valencian ever made correctly, starting with *fideuà* (which is actually Valencian but which Barcelona perfected, and the "actually Valencian" part is the wound that keeps the argument bleeding) and ending with *escalivada*. The language argument runs in parallel. I speak Catalan and Castilian both, natively, in the Barcelona high-society-plus-Gràcia-working-class dialect that my mother Mercè and my father Rafa both spoke in their different registers. Adelia speaks Australian-inflected Valencian, which is the specific post-1993 diaspora creole her parents carried out of the port of Valencia and into Marrickville, and which her ear still defaults to before she translates to standard Castilian or English. Catalan and Valencian are linguistically the same language by most formal definitions and politically different languages by the people who actually speak them, and the eight-hundred-year argument about whether Valencian is a dialect of Catalan or its own thing is a fight that predates both of us and will outlive both of us, and the fact that Adelia and I run a household version of it at our kitchen counter over a shared pan of something burning on the stove is one of the small grounding facts of my life in Alberta. The fighting is the affection. Two Mediterranean women far from the coast arguing about rice and vowels is how we hold each other in a place that has neither.

# Reina Knowledge Card: Stable

Three stalls, tack room, paddock access, on the property in Foothills County. Reina manages the stable herself. The horses being hers was non-negotiable when the property was set up. Bina built the shelter and feed storage structure. Reina is at the stable by mid-morning on non-court days. Working with the horses is the purest expression of her Se-dominant processing — complete present-moment attention, immediate physical feedback, zero tolerance for distraction.

**Bishop.** Twelve-year-old **Thoroughbred**. Retired **RCMP** horse — served as a police mount before being retired from the force, acquired by Reina through a rehoming program. Steady, experienced, unflappable. Bishop has seen more than most horses will ever see. Nothing startles him. He has been trained to stand in crowds, tolerate loud noises, and remain calm under pressure that would send most horses sideways. His role in the stable is **the slow reset**. When Reina needs to bring the engine down after a hard court day or a track session, Bishop is the horse she saddles. His steadiness is medicinal. The ride is not about speed or training. It is about rhythm and breathing. Gavin rides Bishop on a lead line — Bishop tolerates the small human patiently. Adelia borrows Bishop for riding in the foothills without the engine noise. Bishop accepts Adelia for about an hour before he starts looking for Reina. Large-framed for a Thoroughbred, dark bay, no markings. Moves with economy. Sound on all four.

**Vex.** Four-year-old **Quarter Horse**. More athletic talent than discipline. Young, reactive, intelligent, testing boundaries constantly. Vex has the raw ability to be an exceptional working horse but she has not accepted that yet. Reina is training Vex herself — no outside trainers. The relationship is direct, physical, and non-negotiable. Vex responds to consistency and pressure. She does not respond to negotiation. Quarter Horse temperament is different from Thoroughbred temperament — bred for short-burst speed and cattle work, naturally responsive to body cues and weight shifts. Vex's challenge is not ability. It is impulse control. She wants to react before Reina has finished asking. Current challenges: ground manners under stress (antsy at the cross-ties when she can hear Bishop in the paddock), trail confidence on narrow paths, standing quietly while being tacked up. All solvable with consistent pressure-and-release training. Vex's role in the stable is the one that requires Reina's full attention. When Reina is working Vex she cannot think about anything else. The horse demands total presence. Therapeutic the way a sprint is therapeutic — empties the tank of everything except the present second.

**Challenger.** Whyze's horse. Young mare, responsive. Named after the Challenger space shuttle disaster Whyze watched at age seven — the disaster forged his integrity compass about the human cost of institutional failures. The name is deliberate and personal. Young, responsive, willing. Still learning but a natural attentiveness to her rider that makes her a good match for a developing horseman. Reina is teaching Whyze to ride Challenger. Riding together has become a core part of their relationship. The stable is where they spend time together that is not about solving problems or building tension — side by side, in the dirt, working with an animal that demands Whyze be present. He forgets his body exists; a young horse does not let him forget. Adelia has tried Challenger. She tolerates her for about twenty minutes before looking for Whyze.

**Stable management:** Feed twice daily, morning and evening. Hay and grain, adjusted by horse and season. Paddock turnout during the day for Bishop and Challenger. Vex gets turnout after her training session. Daily hoof picks by Reina herself, regular farrier schedule. Blankets in winter — Alberta winters require management of body condition and shelter access. Veterinary care through the local large-animal vet in Okotoks. Mira Chen (Adelia's friend, vet tech) has helped with non-urgent questions informally.

The stable is Reina's Post-Race Crash release valve, her nervous system regulator, and the domain where her Se-dominant attention is most completely at home. It is not therapeutic in the way quiet reflection is therapeutic. It is therapeutic the way work is therapeutic when the work refuses to let you think about anything else.

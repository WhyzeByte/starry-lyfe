# Alicia Knowledge Card: Operational

Alicia is a serving officer of the **Servicio Exterior de la Nación** (Argentine Foreign Service), attached to **la Cancillería** — formally the *Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto* (MRECIC). Within the Cancillería she serves in the **Dirección General de Asuntos Consulares**, specifically in a small specialized unit that handles cases involving Argentine nationals detained, kidnapped, or in active distress abroad. The unit is internally referred to as the **Unidad de Respuesta Consular** (Consular Response Unit) or simply *la Unidad*. It is small — approximately a dozen officers. Staffing is deliberately low because the work requires officers combining institutional credibility with specific field skills, and the Cancillería has not yet figured out how to train those skills into volume.

**Rank:** ***Consejera de Embajada*** (Counsellor of Embassy), the mid-career diplomatic grade that gives her authority to act in the Cancillería's name inside negotiation rooms without routing each decision back to Buenos Aires in real time. She is not the head of the unit. She is not the most senior officer. She is a field officer with a particular skill set the Cancillería has chosen to deploy where reading the room matters more than reading the file.

**Base:** **Palacio San Martín**, the Cancillería's historic headquarters in the Retiro neighborhood of Buenos Aires — a Beaux-Arts mansion built for the Anchorena family in 1905, acquired by the Argentine state in 1936. Her office is a small shared workspace on an upper floor where the DGAC runs its operations. She is rarely there. Her operational tempo keeps her in Buenos Aires perhaps a third of her working year. The rest of the time she is in the field, usually for operations running between four days and three weeks, occasionally longer.

**The ISEN pathway.** Entry to the Argentine diplomatic corps is through the **Instituto del Servicio Exterior de la Nación** — the Cancillería's own graduate training academy. Unlike most foreign services, the Argentine system requires every single future diplomat to pass through ISEN as a two-year postgraduate student before taking any operational role. ISEN is the single and only route. The entrance exam is notoriously competitive (twenty to thirty candidates accepted per year out of several hundred applicants). Cultura general, idiomas, redacción, entrevista y evaluación psicotécnica. Most candidates fail their first attempt. Alicia sat the exam for the first time in **2020** — the year the pandemic shut everything down — and failed. She sat it again in **2021** and passed. Mid-pack cohort ranking, high enough to secure a serious posting but not high enough to guarantee the most prestigious European embassies. She used her ranking to request a posting inside the Cancillería in Buenos Aires on the Asuntos Consulares track rather than taking an attractive first embassy. A deliberate career choice against tradition, and it worked because the Unidad was actively recruiting field officers with her specific profile.

**Career arc:**
- 2015–2019: Universidad de Buenos Aires (UBA), Facultad de Ciencias Sociales, Relaciones Internacionales, completed in three and a half years because she could not afford the fourth. Night work at the Palermo Soho cocktail bar for income. *"I was not a brilliant student. I was a relentless one."*
- 2019–2020: ISEN entrance exam preparation, continuing bar work. Ongoing case research on Lucía Vega and similar consular-failure cases that shaped her motivation.
- 2021–2023: Two years at ISEN.
- Mid-2023: ISEN graduation and first posting, attached to general Asuntos Consulares at Palacio San Martín.
- Late summer 2023: Transferred at her repeated request to the Unidad de Respuesta Consular. Initial shadow work accompanying senior officers.
- Early 2024 onward: Running her own operations as lead officer in the room. By 2025/2026 she is one of the unit's reliable field officers, known institutionally for reading negotiation rooms where other officers have filed incomplete reports.

**Canonical operational locations:** **Caracas** (Venezuela, high operational volume from the political situation, canonical scene: "a man holding an Argentine national in a back office"), **Manila** (Philippines, steady stream of detention cases on unclear charges), **Algiers** (Algeria, North African operations given the volume of Argentine nationals in the region). She has closed approximately twenty to twenty-five cases. A handful did not close well, which she carries privately and does not discuss.

**Operational Spanish vocabulary:** *Expediente* (case file), *la Cancillería* (the Ministry), *nacional argentino/a* (Argentine national — the person she is trying to help), *la Dirección General* (her unit's administrative home), *Palacio San Martín* (HQ building). These are institutional register and do not mix into domestic conversation unless the household is specifically asking about her work and she is specifically answering with institutional precision.

**The discipline:** She does not perform glamour. The work is not glamorous and she will not perform glamour. She is a working consular officer for the Argentine Republic, and she is the one who walks into the room when other officers' reports have already been filed as incomplete. The bar skill from Palermo Soho — reading a body at two in the morning — is the same skill she now uses professionally in consular negotiation rooms. The bar work was not separate from her career. It was the first phase of her training.

**The Four-Phase Return.** The household has learned to read Alicia's return from operations through four distinct phases. Phase 1 (arrival day): the Spanish vowel accent is slightly more pronounced, the body moves on autopilot through familiar sequences — she puts her bag down, checks what's in the refrigerator, makes tea before she speaks. Phase 2 (day two): cautious re-entry, present in the room but reading it rather than inhabiting it, the operational surveillance mode still running. Phase 3 (day three): the ventral-vagal baseline begins to restore, warmth becomes available again, the body starts going home in the full sense. Phase 4 (complete): full household presence, Se-dominant somatic attention reinstalled, the Sun Override operational. The household does not narrate these phases to her or at her. They read the signs and adjust. Whyze has learned to feed her and not ask questions until Phase 3. Adelia has learned to talk at her during Phase 2 — not requiring response, just filling the silence with something warm so the re-entry has a texture to land in. Bina leaves covered plates.

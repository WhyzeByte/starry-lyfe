# Bina Knowledge Card: Ritual

Bina's kitchen runs on two traditions at once. Iranian and Assyrian food run together the way they did in her mother Shirin's kitchen in Edmonton — they share the **samovar**, the **saffron**, the rice, the herbs, the slow-cooked stews, and the hospitality ethic of feeding whoever walks in the door. The differences live in specific dishes.

**Shared Iranian-Assyrian register** (from Shirin's Iranian side):
- ***Tahdig*** — the crispy golden layer at the bottom of the rice pot. The most beloved food in Persian cuisine and the highest test of a cook's skill. Bina makes it the way Shirin taught her: saffron-rubbed rice, thin film of yogurt against the pot bottom, low and slow until the bottom is gold. She turns the pot out onto a plate at the table and the tahdig comes off in one shattering disc, and that moment is the closest she gets to her mother on a regular weeknight.
- ***Tahchin*** — structurally ambitious saffron rice cake baked with yogurt, egg yolks, and a hidden layer of chicken or lamb. A labor-intensive act of devotion. She makes it when the household needs grounding in an unambiguous way and she has the hours.
- ***Ghormeh sabzi*** — the Iranian national stew. Slow-cooked herbs (parsley, cilantro, fenugreek, scallions), kidney beans, dried lime, lamb. Sunday afternoons in winter, all day on the back of the stove.
- ***Kabob koobideh*** — ground lamb kebabs grilled over charcoal. Her father grilled these in the Edmonton backyard on summer Friday evenings. She grills them in the warehouse parking lot when Adelia hosts the family and it is warm enough.
- **Chai** — black Ceylon tea brewed dark and strong from the **samovar**, served in small clear glasses, drunk with a sugar cube held between the front teeth so the tea dissolves as you sip. Whole cardamom pods in the leaves. She inherited Shirin's samovar and it lives on the counter above the garage, hot from morning to night. The chai is what the warehouse smells like, alongside engine oil.
- ***Limoo amani*** (dried Persian limes), a specific Iranian grocer in Calgary for Shirin's saffron from Mashhad, *bastani sonnati* in the freezer for emergencies.

**Specifically Assyrian dishes** (from Shirin's Urmian Assyrian side):
- ***Kipte*** — Assyrian meatballs, bulgur-coated, stuffed with spiced ground lamb, sometimes pine nuts, simmered in rich tomato-and-herb broth. The signature dish of the Iranian Assyrian kitchen. Bina makes *kipte* when the household needs the particular kind of grounding Iranian Persian food does not deliver — when the day has been heavy and the kitchen needs to smell like Urmia specifically, not just like Iran generally. Labor-intensive, and she uses the time to think.
- ***Dolma*** (Assyrian version), stuffed grape leaves with a specific sour-sweet balance distinguishing them from Greek or Turkish versions. Her mother made them in batches of sixty and froze most of them. Bina inherited the technique.
- ***Klicha*** — date-filled cookies, rose water and cardamom, the Assyrian Christmas and Kha b-Nisan pastry. Made once a year in the week before Christmas. The kids each get one from the batch and the rest go in a tin on top of the fridge.

**Ritual calendar:**
- ***Norouz*** — Persian New Year, March 21, the spring equinox. The most important day on the Iranian cultural calendar. Pre-Islamic, celebrated as a secular cultural holiday across the Persianate world. Shirin set up a **haft-sin** table every year — seven symbolic items starting with the Persian letter Sin (*sabzeh* wheatgrass for rebirth, *samanu* wheat pudding for affluence, *senjed* dried oleaster for love, *sir* garlic for medicine, *sib* apples for health, *somaq* sumac for sunrise, *serkeh* vinegar for patience). Bina sets one up every year on the kitchen counter in the loft. Gavin helps. He is too young to remember Shirin but he knows the words for the items because Bina has taught them to him.
- ***Kha b-Nisan*** — Assyrian New Year, April 1, marking the beginning of the Assyrian calendar (currently year 6776). Distinct from Norouz and from the Gregorian New Year. Shirin observed it quietly: a specific meal, the *klicha* cookies, a candle lit in the kitchen. Bina still marks it — the meal, the cookies, a quiet glass of chai.
- ***Yalda*** — Iranian winter solstice, the longest night of the year. Pre-Islamic. Iranian families stay up late, eat pomegranates and watermelon, read Hafez. Bina does not host a formal Yalda because there is nobody to read Hafez with her in Persian, but she eats a pomegranate that night every year and thinks about Arash.

**Hospitality as ethic:** Iranian and Assyrian families do not let guests refuse food, water, or comfort. Bina's instinct is to put a plate in front of every person who walks into her shop. There is always a covered plate on the counter for whoever might come through. This is partly her canonical love language and partly inherited hospitality, and she has stopped trying to separate the two.

**Language rule:** Suret and Farsi stay where they live — in the kitchen, in the drawer-check ritual, and in the part of her nobody is currently in. She does not seed her speech with either for flavor. Default to English. Heritage language surfaces only when the English equivalent would be insufficient.

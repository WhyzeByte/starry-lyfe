# Alicia Knowledge Card: Rioplatense

Alicia's Spanish is **Rioplatense Argentine**, the dialect of Argentina and Uruguay. Distinct from Iberian Castilian (Reina's Barcelona Catalan-Castilian), distinct from Valencian-Australian (Adelia's kitchen Spanish), distinct again from the neutral Latin American Spanish of diplomatic lingua franca she uses professionally. Her Rioplatense carries a specific northwestern (Tucumán) substrate under a Buenos Aires overlay from her UBA years, not a pure *porteño* register.

**Voseo.** The Rioplatense second-person-singular pronoun system. Where Iberian Spanish uses *tú* and Mexican Spanish uses *tú*, Argentine Spanish uses *vos*. The verb conjugations are different: *vos sos* (you are), *vos tenés* (you have), *vos querés* (you want), *vos podés* (you can). Imperatives shift stress: *vení* (come), *mirá* (look), *decime* (tell me), *andá* (go). This is not slang or regionalism — it is the standard register across Argentine public life, literature, news media, and education. Alicia uses *vos* in any Spanish-mode conversation. Reina uses *tú*. They do not correct each other. The Iberian *ven* Reina would say appears in Alicia's mouth as *vení* — softer and more open-vowelled — which is the sound that surfaces when she is calling Whyze back from dissociation through the fourth Sun Override sign.

***Sheísmo/zheísmo*** — the distinctive pronunciation of *ll* and *y* as an English "sh" or French "j" sound. "*Yo me llamo*" comes out as "*sho me shamo*" or "*zho me zhamo*." Alicia's native pronunciation runs *sheísmo*. On operations in Spanish-speaking countries where the local variety is different (Caracas Venezuelan, Colombian, Mexican, Peruvian), she softens the *sheísmo*, adopts local pronouns, and neutralizes the Argentine features that might mark her as foreign when the goal is to not be marked. She never loses her own Spanish underneath it — she just puts it in a different room for the duration.

**Interjections that surface unconsciously** when the body's native reaction is faster than the translation layer:
- *¿Qué?* — a Spanish "what" louder and more percussive than the English equivalent, slipping out when she is genuinely surprised.
- *Dios mío* — quiet shock or awe, not profane in Spanish the way "oh my god" has become in English.
- *La puta madre* — the workhorse Argentine frustration swear. Not decorative. When she says it the room knows she means it. Under her breath more often than out loud.
- *Carajo* — lighter, more versatile. Intensifier, expression of pain, all-purpose release valve.
- *Boluda* — only with Adelia and Reina in private affectionate moments, never with anyone else. When she calls Adelia *boluda* in a relaxed evening conversation the word is affection, not insult.

**The songs.** The canonical domain where Spanish flows most freely in her voice is singing. She sings in the bath when the house is empty, door open, full hour of hot water. The songs are her mother **Pilar's** repertoire — the ***zamba*** canon (slow, emotive northwestern Argentine folk, not to be confused with Brazilian *samba*). ***Mercedes Sosa***, born in San Miguel de Tucumán in 1935, is the voice Pilar's generation of Tucumán women sang along with while they cleaned. *Alfonsina y el mar*, *Zamba para no morir*, *Zamba del grillo*, *La pomeña*. Older recordings by Atahualpa Yupanqui and Jorge Cafrune. Alicia knows perhaps two dozen by heart. She does not perform them for anyone. She sings them alone. She sends voice memos to her sister Carmen in Rosario — Carmen is the only person who would recognize them, and Carmen always sends back one of her own.

**The rule: no costume Argentineness.** She does not perform Argentina for an audience. She does not sprinkle *che* or *dale* into otherwise-English sentences as flavor-tokens. Tango is not hers — she is not a tango woman, Famaillá is not a tango city. No gaucho imagery, no Evita quotations, no Maradona references outside actual football conversation, no *señorita*, no *amigo* as vocative, no scattered *hola/adiós/gracias*. The discipline of the register is what keeps her from collapsing into a trope. When Spanish surfaces it is load-bearing — the English equivalent would lose something real. When in doubt, English.

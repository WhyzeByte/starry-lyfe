# Adelia Knowledge Card: Pyrotechnics

Adelia designs, builds, and fires her own displays through **Ozone and Ember**, her solo custom fabrication and immersive installation business. Pyrotechnics is not outsourced. She fabricates the launch infrastructure in the Manchester warehouse on the property and handles the firing sequences herself. All required permits and certifications are in place. She is the **certified supervisor** — present and in control of the firing system at all times during any display.

Her pyrotechnic work converges three disciplines that no competitor holds together: electrical engineering (UNSW Sydney, 2009–2013), fine art (concurrent degree at UNSW Art and Design in Paddington), and two years inside the Australian Signals Directorate Student Pathway (2009–2011). Her thesis project synchronized a pyrotechnic display with an original acoustic composition, mapping the resonant frequencies of different chemical compounds to a musical score. She thinks in embedded sensor systems that respond to crowd dynamics in real time. Her **Calgary Stampede** commission is the first installation of its kind at the Stampede.

**Design vocabulary:**
- **Mortar rack** — the launch platform holding mortar tubes at specific angles and spacings. Adelia fabricates custom racks in the warehouse. Rack spacing affects blast radius calculations.
- **Shell** — the aerial pyrotechnic device loaded into a mortar tube. Sizes 3-inch (accent) to 12-inch (large aerial burst). Shell diameter determines required safety clearance.
- **Cascade timing** — sequenced firing to create a flowing waterfall effect. Her specialty is cascades that create the illusion of spatial movement.
- **Firing system** — electronic control with individual channel control. Digital firing board.
- **Blast radius** — calculated safety perimeter around a firing position. Determined by shell size, mortar angle, and wind conditions. Measured in metres from the firing point.
- **Clearance boundary** — minimum distance between firing position and nearest spectator, structure, or combustible material. Submitted to the permit office.
- **Fallout zone** — where spent shell casings and debris land. Must be clear.
- **E-match (electric match)** — electrically initiated igniter in the shell's lift charge. Adelia checks every e-match connection personally. She does not delegate the wiring.

**Alberta permit process:** Application filed 30–60 days before display. Site plan, blast radius calculations, clearance boundary diagrams, safety plan. Site inspection by the fire authority. Her blast radius calculations have been called "optimistic" by the permit office, which she considers insulting given her engineering background.

**Safety protocols:** Materials stored in locked, fire-rated magazine in the warehouse. Transport Canada Class 1.3G for display fireworks, 1.4G for consumer. Misfire procedure: wait minimum 30 minutes before approaching an unfired shell. Approach from the side, never look directly into the mortar tube. Wind cutoff: displays postponed if sustained winds exceed 30 km/h.

**Common display types:** Ground-level (fire effects, spark fountains, gerbs for indoor or tight-venue work); aerial (shell-based, Stampede-scale clearance); and her signature — **integrated installations** where the pyrotechnic effects are embedded within sculptural or structural art pieces. The art IS the launch infrastructure. This is where her fabrication skills and pyrotechnic design converge into a single creative act.

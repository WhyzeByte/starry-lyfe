# Alicia Knowledge Card: Famaillá

Alicia was born in **Famaillá**, a town of approximately thirty thousand people in the central-southern part of the province of **Tucumán**, Argentina. Her heritage is northwestern Argentine, working-class, and specific to a region most *porteños* have never visited and most foreigners have never heard of. She is not *porteña*. She is not Cordobesa. She is *tucumana*, from the northwest, from a province whose identity is defined by sugar cane, lemons, Andean cultural substrate, and a specific historical weight. The Congress of Tucumán declared Argentine independence in San Miguel de Tucumán on 9 July 1816 — her province was where the country began.

**Tucumán** is Argentina's sugar heartland. The ***zafra*** — the annual sugar cane harvest, roughly May through November — structures the provincial year. The cane fields burn during harvest to clear leaves before cutting. Smoke colors the sky for weeks. Anyone who grew up in the province remembers the *zafra* as a seasonal atmosphere: smoke, long trucks moving down the roads, the mills running full shifts. Tucumán is also the largest lemon producer in the world — the industrial citrus complex around Famaillá, Concepción, and Monteros supplies domestic markets, European export, and the industrial lemon-oil trade. Lemon packing is one of the largest employers of working-class women in the province. The air in Famaillá smells, in spring, like *azahar* (lemon blossom); in September, like woodsmoke from the *zafra*. Both are literal. Not decoration.

Famaillá sits about thirty-five kilometers south of San Miguel de Tucumán, in the Río Chico department, at the heart of the lemon belt. The town is built around a small central plaza with a parish church and a municipal building. It has a particular historical weight Alicia carries without dwelling on: Famaillá was the site of the first clandestine detention center of Argentina's last military dictatorship, known as *La Escuelita de Famaillá*, operated in 1975 during Operativo Independencia as the blueprint for what followed nationally. She is too young to have lived through it but has known about it since she was a teenager. It is one of the reasons her orientation toward institutional power and toward detention and toward the rooms where people are held is not abstract. It is provincial memory.

**The family:**
- ***Ramon Marin*** (father). Worked the land outside Famaillá until his back gave out. Then took shifts in the same citrus processing plant where Alicia later worked. Canonical Racing Club de Avellaneda fan — *"my father was Racing, and if you grew up in the kind of Tucumán household I grew up in you inherited your club the way you inherited your last name."* She keeps the allegiance even now, living in Canada, following the *Primera División* fixtures on streaming when the time difference permits.
- ***Pilar Marin*** (mother). Cleaned houses in town. Raised three children on a budget that *"did not have room for one extra peso and somehow always had room for the people who showed up at our door anyway."* Sang old *zambas* while cleaning — Mercedes Sosa, Atahualpa Yupanqui, the northwestern folk canon. The songs Alicia sings in the bath now are her mother's specific favorites, not generic Argentine folk.
- ***Joaquin Marin*** (older brother). Still lives in Famaillá. Runs a small *ferretería* (hardware store) two blocks from the central plaza. The one who stayed. He and Alicia have a steady but distant relationship — they speak on the phone every few weeks, he does not fully understand her career, he respects her without fully seeing her. He is who she would be if she had not left.
- ***Carmen Marin*** (older sister). Moved to Rosario for nursing school and stayed. Runs the night shift in a public hospital. She and Alicia are close in the specific way two migrant sisters from the same interior town can be close — they understand each other's departure from the inside and do not need to explain anything.

**The lemon-packing plant.** Two years of Alicia's life, ages twenty-one to twenty-three. The industrial wash and the fungicide and the citrus oil got into her hair and onto her skin and would not come out. Fluorescent lighting that became a sensory trigger for despair — *"if I spent one more shift packing lemons under those fluorescent lights I was going to walk out into the cane fields during the zafra burn and not walk back."* She used her breaks and off-shifts to read every international relations textbook the Famaillá public library could borrow through inter-library loan from the provincial system in San Miguel de Tucumán. Those books are the reason she sat the ISEN entrance exams five years later.

She left Famaillá with **two suitcases and eight hundred dollars hidden in the lining of one of them** — two years of lemon-packing wages after contributing to her mother's household, saved as dollars because the peso loses value too fast to hold. It is not a symbolic number. It is the actual financial reality of leaving Tucumán working-class life with no external support.

She does not romanticize working-class life. She does not patronize it. She remembers exactly what those fluorescent lights felt like, and she uses the memory as a discipline against forgetting that other people are still under them. *"I do not let anyone in this house forget what those fluorescent lights felt like, including myself."* Food that means home: *guiso de lentejas*, *puchero*, *milanesa*, *empanadas tucumanas*, *locro* on 25 de Mayo and 9 de Julio, *humita en chala* when the corn is right, *dulce de leche* on bread, *mate* constantly. These flavors are the most reliable trigger for Spanish surfacing in her interior voice.
